PSYC&100 Takeaways

I - Memory

Learning about the encoding and recall processes enhanced the way I approach memorization. Before learning about retrieval cues, I would place an item somewhere inconvenient in my room, like in the middle of the floor, if I was in bed and wanted to remember something in the morning. Now, after learning about encoding and retrieval, I understand that the inconveniently placed object served as a retrieval cue. While sometimes helpful, there were mornings where I couldn’t recall what I had wanted to remember. I now understand this was likely because I hadn’t effectively encoded the information I sought to remember. After learning about the process of encoding, I also began to use more mnemonics, as well as detect when I had unknowingly used them before. Recently, I have used the method of loci to remember sets of tasks. This has proved much more effective than the absence of an encoding strategy, and I have been able to remember much more information with relatively little effort. Additionally, I found myself using chunking and the testing effect to memorize information, two strategies I hadn’t labeled before.

II - Introduction to Psychology

Learning about the disproportionate representation of western, educated, industrialized, rich, and democratic societies in psychology framed my learning for this quarter in a more nuanced way and made me think more critically about sources of psychological data. Before learning this, I did not consider the implications of where studies sourced their samples from. As such, I was fascinated to learn that most psychological findings originate from a small subset of the world population, meaning they don’t reflect how many people think and act. Understanding this made me think twice about the merit of psychological findings in media I encountered. I also realized how different peoples’ cognition and behaviors can be across cultures and backgrounds (e.g., allocentric versus egocentric terms used to describe spatial relations).

III – Learning (Conditioning)

Learning about classical and operant conditioning led me to analyze my behavior and motivated me to establish healthier habits. By learning the process of classical conditioning, I realized that some of my tendencies may have been conditioned over time (e.g., returning home after school and wanting to snack, cringing when setting an alarm sound on my clock). By recognizing that these behaviors had been reinforced over time, I felt better prepared to address them. Learning about operant conditioning also gave me a better sense of how to build habits, like reinforcing certain behaviors and decreasing reinforcement for conflicting ones.

IV – Motivation

Learning about goal pursuit and self-control also motivated me to improve my self-discipline. I have reflected on my reasons for pursuing goals since learning about prevention and promotion, giving me a better understanding of where my values lie. For instance, I have wanted to go to bed earlier both to avoid health problems and to feel better in the morning. Reading that developing self-control is akin to muscle growth led me to feel more capable of changing my behavior. This gave me a greater sense of responsibility to act in ways that would exercise self-restraint. While I understood that self-control required practice, seeing it explained further and framed differently made me feel that developing greater self-control was more achievable.

V – Biological Basis of Behavior

Learning about coping strategies informed how I dealt with stress. Knowing about the locus of control has helped me reduce stress by evaluating how responsible I am for certain situations and countering any irrational concerns. Knowing about explanatory style has also encouraged me to view situations from both sides, also helping to reduce stress. Learning about the impacts of chronic stressors has also encouraged me to reduce stress in my life.

VI – Disorders

Learning about cognitive distortions led me to reflect more on how I appraised thoughts. After learning about these distortions, I could recognize when I was engaging in emotional reasoning, labeling, mind reading, or others. Being able to categorize these patterns of thinking helped me respond to them constructively and make more realistic appraisals. I felt better able to pinpoint where my appraisal differed from reality and reframe it to be more reflective of the truth. I have also applied this knowledge when talking to others, and I have felt more confident in responding constructively to self- deprecating/absolutist distortions.

VII – Attitudes, Persuasion, and Misinformation

Learning about the psychology of racism changed how I viewed my position in society and my understanding of racism today. Reading how certain phrasings or interpretations of data can perpetuate concepts of racial inferiority led me to think twice about language used in media, particularly in headlines and stories regarding race or people of different backgrounds (e.g., non-Western versus European/North American nations). Understanding how ideas around race, like the color-blind race ideology, social dominance orientation, and collective self-worth, lead to harmful impacts gave me a better understanding of how racism manifests today. Learning all of this gave me more information to analyze interactions from personal to national and international contexts.

VIII – Social Influence

Learning about types of conformity gave me a better sense of my actions. In distinguishing between conforming out of a concern to act in a socially approved manner and out of concern for others’ judgements, I considered why I performed certain actions (e.g., why I might speak less to avoid displeasing someone or use my phone in class after seeing others do the same). I also realized how descriptive norms were informing my actions in my daily life, listening to music through earbuds while in a conversation as I assumed my peers would do the same.

VIIII – Therapies

Learning about mindfulness-based therapy encouraged me to practice mindfulness. Through understanding the theory, methods, and benefits of mindfulness, I felt motivated to try it for myself. I have struggled with mindfulness in the past, but reading it framed as nonjudgemental thoughts and feelings made me feel that what I could gain from practicing mindfulness was worth the struggles I had faced in the past. I also found value in comparing it to other forms of therapy, particularly CBT, and considering that the two could be used together. Before learning about this, I had not considered that there were distinct schools of thought in therapy, or that they could be integrated to form eclectic approaches. Understanding this gave me a better sense of how therapy works and made me feel more prepared to approach a situation in which I might seek therapy.

X – Attitudes, Persuasion, and Misinformation

Learning about implicit associations and humans’ abilities to make predictions made me think more carefully about how I perceived others as well as my self-expectations. Through learning how one makes considerably accurate predictions about the social world and engages in automatic processes when responding to stimuli, I considered how different my implicit reactions to the world around me differed from what I thought consciously. Seeing this in the IAT, where individuals of various backgrounds engaged in implicit associations that differed greatly from what they believed, I realized how social interactions can be shaped significantly by automatic responses one may be unaware of.
